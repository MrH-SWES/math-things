# Math Things. Updated Design Doctrine

> Math Things. | Math, in your hands.

## 1. Core Identity

Math Things are digital manipulatives for K–5 classrooms.

A Math Thing is not a standalone lesson, tutor, worksheet, quiz, dashboard, or game with math pasted on top. It is a physical-feeling mathematical object that a teacher can place in front of students so the mathematics becomes something to move, split, join, compare, measure, sort, rotate, bundle, unbundle, transform, or build.

The teacher teaches. The tool gives the teacher something worth teaching with.

The student interface should stay quiet. Teacher-facing lesson plans, prompts, standards alignment, and discussion moves live around the app, not inside it.

---

## 2. Prime Directive: No Words, No Directions

The first obvious interaction should be mathematically meaningful.

- Do not explain the math through instructions.
- Do not rely on tutorial text.
- Do not make the student read before they can act.
- Do not use badges, points, or game mechanics unless they preserve the mathematical structure.
- Do not place procedural explanations inside the student interface.

Labels may appear only when they are part of the mathematical object itself: numerals on a number line, values on place-value pieces, tick marks, coordinate labels, equation records, or teacher-toggleable symbolic layers.

The mathematics should live in the constraints of the interface.

---

## 3. The Object Comes First

Every Math Thing must make one mathematical abstraction physical.

Examples:

- Ten ones physically become one ten.
- A fraction cannot combine with another fraction until the wholes are partitioned compatibly.
- A multiplication product appears as area.
- A coordinate is a place reached by horizontal and vertical movement.
- A rounded number is the valley a bead rolls toward.
- A clock is a fraction circle, angle dial, and time machine.
- A sorted category becomes a count, and a count becomes a comparison.
- A line can be pulled into a grid.
- A grid can be pulled into a solid.

The equation is the shadow of the object, not the object itself.

Symbols, equations, labels, and algorithms may appear, but they should usually be optional, delayed, teacher-toggleable, or visually subordinate to the object.

---

## 4. The Boundaries of the Interface Are the Boundaries of the Math

A Math Thing should not tell students what is allowed. It should make allowed moves possible and disallowed moves impossible, resistant, incomplete, or visibly unstable.

Correct structure should snap, fit, fuse, align, reveal, settle, glide, lock, or transform.

Incorrect structure should resist, fail visibly, stay incomplete, or remain unmerged.

If students can succeed while misunderstanding the target structure, the design needs work.

---

## 5. Whimsy Rule

Whimsy is allowed when it carries the math.

A flying pegamole can make coordinates feel like navigation. A rounding wave can make nearest feel like gravity. A prime altar can make factorization feel like discovery. A clock dial can become time, fractions, and angle measure at once.

Decoration is optional. Metaphor is powerful. The question is whether the student understands the math better because of it.

---

## 6. Product Families

### Core Manipulatives

Flexible, teacher-directed objects used across many lessons: ten-frames, rekenreks, number bonds, place-value cards, base-ten blocks, fraction bars, clocks, number lines, and construction tools.

### Concept Machines

Narrower tools that embody a specific structure: rounding waves, partial products, common grids, sorters, coordinate quests, prime altars, and fraction/angle dials.

### Bridges

Tools that connect physical reasoning to symbolic recording: regrouping to algorithms, arrays to multiplication, fraction alignment to notation, and division pieces to long division.

### Algorithm Machines

Procedure-focused tools are separate from core manipulatives. A free-play dimensional manipulative should not become an algorithm trainer. Addition/subtraction and multiplication algorithm machines can exist, but they should be separate apps or separate clearly scoped tools.

---

## 7. Dimensional Builder Doctrine

Dimensional Builder is one manipulative with multiple dimensional permissions, not three unrelated apps.

Modes:

- **Line:** pull quantity along the x-axis.
- **Grid:** pull a line along the y-axis to make an array.
- **Solid:** pull a grid along the z-axis to make a rectangular prism.

The app may eventually expose Line, Grid, and Solid as a minimal three-way mode control. These are not grade levels and not lessons. They are permissions for which axes the student can pull.

A first grader may explore Solid mode without formal volume language. A fifth grader may return to Line mode. The point is to build tactile memory before symbolic vocabulary.

### Core Dimensional Gesture

- Pull right: create length.
- Pull down: repeat length into area.
- Pull up/back: repeat area into volume.

The object should remain directly manipulable. The record follows the object (Line: n; Grid: x × y = area; Solid: x × y × z = volume). The object drives the notation. The notation does not drive the object.

### No Dispenser as Primary Interaction

Dimensional Builder is not a tray/dispenser app. The primary action is pulling structure into existence along an axis. Optional alternate controls may exist for accessibility or teacher use, but the core student-facing action should be direct pulling, resizing, and transforming.

### Place Value Toggle

Dimensional Builder has a shared wordless Place Value toggle.

When Place Value is off, structures remain fully expanded as repeated smaller units.

When Place Value is on, complete groups of ten fuse into the next place-value object:

- 10 ones become one ten-stick.
- 10 ten-sticks become one hundred-flat.
- 10 hundred-flats become one thousand-cube.

Fusion never erases ancestry: ten-sticks still show unit divisions, hundred-flats still show row/column seams, solids still show layer structure where useful.

### Alternating Tens Structure

The Number Line / Line mode should preserve the alternating ten-zone structure inspired by physical classroom number lines. It should support a continuous/global count above the track, a repeating local 1–10 count below each ten-zone, and alternating ten-zone surfaces so 10, 20, 30, and beyond are visually chunked.

---

## 8. Tool Chest Doctrine

The Tool Chest is the product navigation object. It should feel like a premium physical cabinet in a showroom/workshop space.

It is not a web menu, grid of cards, dashboard, or accordion. Drawers are physical objects. Opening a drawer should feel deliberate, heavy, and premium — slow start, controlled acceleration, long glide, gentle heavy settle, no bouncy easing.

Drawer bodies and internal hardware should read as brushed steel or machined metal. Cabinet frame can remain dark graphite/black metal. Drawer faceplates are colored enamel.

---

## 9. Visual Material System

Math Things should feel more physical than ordinary digital UI because the objects are not actually tangible. Digital manipulatives must overperform tactility.

### Mathematical Material Objects

Colored mathematical pieces should read as enameled cast iron, like Le Creuset made base-ten blocks.

They should feel: dense, heavy, durable, premium, color-saturated, rounded but not squishy, glossy but not plastic, manufactured rather than drawn.

They are not candy, jelly, flat UI tiles, toy plastic, or cheap classroom clip art.

Important details: rounded enamel edges, slight bevels, controlled specular highlights, heavy contact shadows, subtle darkening at seams and contact points, engraved or recessed unit divisions where useful.

### Infrastructure Material

Infrastructure should read as brushed or machined metal: beads, handles, rails, tracks, switches, cabinet bodies, drawer cases, axis guides, camera/control hardware.

The bead is neutral brushed aluminum. It is a border-crossing object and should never be assigned a place-value color.

### Surfaces and Sets

Surfaces should feel premium, warm, and quiet: warm showroom/workshop neutrals, soft light background, subtle glass or ceramic surfaces, brushed metal tracks. The colored math objects should be the saturated elements. Infrastructure should support them rather than compete with them.

**Shorthand:** Le Creuset made the blocks; Snap-on built the cabinet; Apple shot the product video.

| Tenths | `#64D2FF` |
| Hundredths | `#FF9F0A` |
| Thousandths | `#7B00FF` |
| The Bead | brushed aluminum / neutral gray |

Whole-number places grow leftward from ones. Decimal places shrink rightward from ones. Color should reinforce structure, not decorate randomly.

## 11. Motion Doctrine

Motion should teach.

Use motion to communicate: fit, resistance, transformation, equivalence, scale, grouping, belonging, fusion, mass, friction, settle.

Default spring for light snap/settling: `cubic-bezier(0.34, 1.56, 0.64, 1)`

But not every movement should be springy. Heavy objects need heavy motion.

For enameled cast-iron blocks:
- units should pop or thud into existence,
- disappearing units should feel removed, not erased,
- fusing ten into a ten-stick should have a larger, heavier settle,
- feedback should be tactile but not cartoonish.

If haptics are available, subtle mobile vibration can support tactile events — small pulse for unit creation, slightly larger pulse for ten-stick fusion — restrained and respectful of reduced-motion preferences.

---

## 12. Interaction Rules

1. The obvious action should be the mathematically useful action.
2. Correct structure should snap, fit, fuse, align, reveal, or settle.
3. Incorrect structure should resist, fail visibly, or remain incomplete.
4. Avoid text explanations inside the student interface.
5. Use labels only when they are part of the mathematical object, not directions.
6. Make symbols optional, delayed, teacher-toggleable, or secondary when possible.
7. Prefer direct manipulation over typed input.
8. Prefer physical constraints over answer checking.
9. Prefer one clear mathematical object over many representations at once.
10. If the app tries to do too much, split it into smaller tools.
11. Student-facing mode controls should be minimal and preferably wordless.
12. Gesture controls should feel like manipulating the object, not operating a dashboard.

---

## 13. Development Constraints

- Build as a single-file HTML/CSS/JS prototype unless otherwise specified.
- Use Lexend.
- Prioritize touch interaction.
- Design for classroom projection and student tablet use.
- Keep student-facing text minimal.
- Use teacher-facing notes outside the app, not inside the app.
- Avoid external libraries unless explicitly approved or clearly justified by the physical interaction.
- Use Three.js when the object is genuinely three-dimensional and CSS/DOM transform stacks become fragile.

Every app footer may use: **Math Things. | Math, in your hands.**

## 14. Audit Rubric

Before calling an app finished, ask:

1. What is the mathematical object?
2. What is one?
3. What action does the student take?
4. What structure does the action reveal?
5. Can the student succeed without engaging the intended math?
6. Is there instruction text doing work the interface should do?
7. Does the object feel physical enough?
8. Does motion clarify the math?
9. Does the record follow the object?
10. Could this be simpler?
11. Does the app stay in its lane?

Audit outcomes:

- **Keep:** the tool already enforces the standard cleanly and should remain in the core catalog.
- **Spin / Modify:** the mechanic is promising but needs a variant, constraint change, or narrower use case.
- **Rework:** the concept points at the right standard, but the present design needs substantial restructuring.
- **New Build:** the current tool does not meaningfully enforce the standard. Build from scratch.
