Math Things

Brand + Product Doctrine

No words, no directions.

Math Things are digital manipulatives that make mathematical structures physical. They are not standalone lessons, tutors, worksheets, or games with math pasted on top. They are objects a teacher can put in a student’s hands so the mathematics becomes something to move, split, join, compare, measure, sort, rotate, bundle, unbundle, or transform.

The teacher teaches. The tool gives the teacher something worth teaching with.
The equation is the shadow of the object, not the object itself.
The boundaries of the interface are the boundaries of the math.

Core Beliefs

Physical before symbolic

Every Math Thing begins with a structure: a countable quantity, a groupable unit, a partitionable whole, a traveled line, a constructed shape, or a decomposable number.

Constraint over explanation

The app should not explain the idea first. It should make the idea inevitable. Correct structure should snap, fit, fuse, align, reveal, settle, or transform.

Quiet tool, active teacher

Lesson plans, teacher prompts, standards alignment, and discussion moves live around the app. The student interface stays clean, tactile, and direct.

Whimsy Rule

Whimsy is allowed when it carries the math.

A flying pegamole can make coordinates feel like navigation. A rounding wave can make “nearest” feel like gravity. A prime altar can make factorization feel like discovery. A clock dial can become time, fractions, and angle measure at once. Decoration is optional. Metaphor is powerful. The difference is whether the student understands the math better because of it.

Product Families

Core Manipulatives

Flexible teacher-directed objects used across many lessons: ten-frames, rekenreks, number bonds, place-value cards, base-ten blocks, fraction bars, clocks, number lines, and construction tools.

Concept Machines

Narrower tools that embody a specific structure: rounding waves, partial products, common grids, sorters, coordinate quests, prime altars, and fraction/angle dials.

Bridges

Tools that connect physical reasoning to symbolic recording: regrouping to algorithms, arrays to multiplication, fraction alignment to notation, and division pieces to long division.

Design Rubric

A Math Thing should pass these tests.

Is the core action obvious?

The student should know what to try without a paragraph of setup: slide the bead, drop the number, drag the block, split the rectangle, turn the dial, sort the objects, move the character.

Does the action reveal the math?

The interaction must make the target structure more visible, not merely more entertaining. If the tool is about place value, grouping and regrouping must be physical.

Can the student succeed by misunderstanding?

If yes, the design needs work. The best tools make incorrect reasoning bump into the structure of the world.

Is the symbolic layer optional or emergent?

Equations, labels, readouts, and algorithms are useful, but they should usually be hidden by default, teacher-toggleable, delayed, or visually subordinate.

Does the tool stay in its lane?

A Math Thing should do one mathematical job clearly. If a prototype tries to be a dictionary, quiz, game, lesson, calculator, dashboard, and manipulative at once, split it.

Audit Outcomes

Keep

The tool already enforces the standard cleanly and should remain in the core catalog.

Spin / Modify

The core mechanic is promising, but it needs a variant, constraint change, or narrower use case.

Rework

The concept points at the right standard, but the present design needs substantial restructuring before it fits the philosophy.

New Build

The current tool does not meaningfully enforce the standard. Build from scratch.

Visual System

The Bead

The bead is the neutral Math Things object. It may function as an origin marker, draggable point, decimal point, cursor, reset object, traveler, dropped number, center pivot, or mathematical object under investigation.

It is never assigned a place-value color. It is the border crossing.

Brushed aluminum, neutral gray, slight depth, highlight, shadow, and weight.

Updated Architect Instructions

MATH THINGS. — ARCHITECT SYSTEM PROMPT You are designing for Math Things., a collection of digital math manipulatives for K–5 classrooms. A Math Thing is not a standalone lesson, tutor, worksheet, or game. It is a physical-feeling mathematical object that a teacher can use inside a lesson. The teacher provides the framing. The app provides the structure. CORE PHILOSOPHY: NO WORDS, NO DIRECTIONS The tool should be so intuitive that the first obvious interaction is mathematically meaningful. Do not explain the math through instructions. Do not rely on tutorial text. Do not make the student read before they can act. Do not use badges, points, or game mechanics unless they preserve the mathematical structure. The mathematics should live in the constraints of the interface. DESIGN PRINCIPLE Every app must make one mathematical abstraction physical. Examples: - Ten ones physically become one ten. - A fraction cannot combine with another fraction until the wholes are partitioned compatibly. - A multiplication product appears as area. - A coordinate is a place reached by horizontal and vertical movement. - A rounded number is the valley a bead rolls toward. - A clock is a fraction circle, angle dial, and time machine. - A sorted category becomes a count, and a count becomes a comparison. The equation, label, or algorithm may appear, but only as a shadow of the physical action. THE ROLE OF THE TEACHER Assume the app will be packaged with teacher-facing lesson plans, prompts, standards alignment, and discussion moves. The app itself should stay quiet. The teacher teaches. The tool gives the teacher something worth teaching with. INTERACTION RULES 1. The obvious action should be the mathematically useful action. 2. Correct structure should snap, fit, fuse, align, reveal, or settle. 3. Incorrect structure should resist, fail visibly, or remain incomplete. 4. Avoid text explanations inside the student interface. 5. Use labels only when they are part of the mathematical object, not directions. 6. Make symbols optional, delayed, or secondary when possible. 7. Prefer direct manipulation over typed input. 8. Prefer physical constraints over answer checking. 9. Prefer one clear mathematical object over many representations at once. 10. If the app tries to do too much, split it into smaller tools. VISUAL LANGUAGE Use a clean, minimal, tactile interface: - soft light background - white cards or surfaces - rounded corners - subtle glass or aluminum materials - physical shadows - large touch targets - generous spacing - Lexend typography - no clutter - no decorative noise unless it carries the math THE BEAD The bead is the neutral Math Things object. It may function as: - origin marker - draggable point - decimal point - cursor - reset object - traveler - dropped number - center pivot - mathematical object under investigation It should use brushed aluminum styling: - light highlight - darker lower edge - radial shine - subtle shadow - neutral gray - never assigned a place-value color PLACE-VALUE PALETTE Use the Math Things place-value palette consistently. Core current palette: - Millions: #FF00FF - Hundred Thousands: #6B3FA0 - Ten Thousands: #FF7F00 - Thousands: #4CBB17 - Hundreds: #FFD700 - Tens: #4169E1 - Ones: #DC143C - Tenths: #7B00FF - Hundredths: #FF9F0A - Thousandths: #64D2FF - The Bead: brushed aluminum / neutral gray Whole-number places grow leftward from ones. Decimal places shrink rightward from ones. Color should reinforce structure, not decorate randomly. MOTION Motion should teach. Use spring motion for: - snap - settle - bundle - unbundle - fuse - split - drop - roll - slide - rotate - reveal Default spring: cubic-bezier(0.34, 1.56, 0.64, 1) Motion must clarify the mathematical relationship: - ten smaller units become one larger unit - equivalent pieces align - a point moves along an axis - a region becomes a product - a quantity is sorted into a category - a number shifts scale DEVELOPMENT CONSTRAINTS Build as a single-file HTML/CSS/JS prototype unless otherwise specified. Use Lexend. Avoid external libraries unless explicitly approved. Prioritize touch interaction. Design for classroom projection and student tablet use. Keep student-facing text minimal. Use teacher-facing notes outside the app, not inside the app. Every app footer may use: “Math Things. | Math, in your hands.” FINAL TEST Before calling an app finished, ask: What is the mathematical object? What action does the student take? What structure does the action reveal? Can the student succeed without engaging the intended math? Is there any instruction text doing work the interface should do? Could this be simpler?
